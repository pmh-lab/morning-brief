Global HeadlinesDisaster

Venezuela's twin earthquakes kill more than 1,700 as rescuers still pull survivors from the rubble

A Mw 7.2 foreshock and a Mw 7.5 mainshock 39 seconds apart on 24 June flattened parts of La Guaira and Caracas. The confirmed death toll has now climbed past 1,700, with more than 5,000 injured and tens of thousands reported missing. Forty-four international search-and-rescue teams — some 2,245 specialists and 140 dogs — are still extracting people alive.

▸ The fuller picture

The two strike-slip quakes struck the densely populated northern coast within a minute of each other, collapsing apartment blocks and infrastructure before residents could react. Early counts in the low hundreds rose steeply through the week as crews reached buried structures; UN agencies now put deaths above 1,700 and missing in the tens of thousands, a figure that may yet move.

The international response has been unusually large for the hemisphere: dozens of urban search-and-rescue teams deployed under UN coordination, with rescuers reporting live extractions days into the operation — the window in which collapsed-building survivors are still routinely found. Medical triage and clean water are now the binding constraints as the effort shifts from rescue toward relief.

A preliminary satellite assessment estimates roughly $6.7 billion in direct physical damage — about 6% of Venezuelan GDP — landing on an economy with little fiscal cushion. The reconstruction bill, and how much outside aid flows to a politically isolated government, will shape the recovery long after the cameras leave.

Economic MovesTrade & Growth

World Bank sees global growth sinking to a pandemic-era low as tariff probes multiply

The Bank's June Global Economic Prospects cuts world growth to 2.5% in 2026 — the weakest pace since the pandemic — down from 2.9% in 2025. The WTO expects merchandise-trade growth to crawl at about 1.9%. Layered on top: a new US investigation into some 60 countries threatening tariffs of 10–12.5%, keeping trade policy a live source of uncertainty.

▸ The fuller picture

The headline downgrade is less about any single shock than the accumulation of them. The Middle East conflict, persistent policy uncertainty and a hardening tariff regime are each shaving fractions off growth; together they pull the global number to its lowest reading since COVID. The WTO's 1.9% trade forecast tells the same story from the goods side — world commerce is barely expanding.

The structural shift matters more than the decimal points. Tariffs have migrated from a negotiating tactic to a standing feature of policy, and firms are responding by trading cost-efficiency for resilience: more suppliers, fatter inventory buffers, higher working capital. The fresh US probe into roughly 60 trading partners — flagging duties of 10–12.5% — extends that uncertainty into the back half of the year.

There is a genuine offset, though. Robust AI-related investment and booming chip exports are propping up several economies — South Korea's growth alone is expected to get a ~0.7-point lift from AI-chip demand. The question for 2027, when forecasters pencil in a modest rebound, is whether that capex wave broadens into the rest of the economy or stays concentrated in a narrow band of technology.

Interest RatesCentral Banks

Central banks split: the ECB hikes, the Fed holds, and the BoJ leans toward the yen

June's run of decisions left an unusually divided picture. The ECB raised rates 25bps on 11 June — deposit facility to 2.25%, its first hike since 2023 — citing Middle East-driven inflation. The Fed held on 17 June at 3.50–3.75% amid a sharp internal split, with inflation still above target on energy shocks. The Bank of Japan, meanwhile, is signalling it may tighten to defend a weak yen.

▸ The fuller picture

The Hormuz energy shock has fractured the rate-setting consensus that held through much of the easing cycle. The ECB's hike — reversing eight straight cuts — frames inflation risk from energy and supply disruption as the dominant threat, and the Governing Council described the move as robust across scenarios for how the shock might evolve.

The Fed is the mirror image: paralysed between a cooling economy that argues for cuts and energy-driven inflation that argues against them, the FOMC stood pat with notable dissent. Holding at 3.50–3.75% buys time to see which force wins, but it also means policy is doing little to lean against either.

For markets the divergence is the trade. A tightening ECB against a paused Fed and a BoJ edging toward a yen-defence move reshapes euro–dollar, the carry trade, and the relative cost of capital across the major blocs — a backdrop worth watching as the next round of meetings approaches in late July.

Dow closes above 52,000 for the first time as a tech surge lifts Wall Street

US stocks opened the week with a broad rally on Monday, 29 June. The Dow cleared 52,000 for the first time — helped by Alphabet's debut as a Dow member, where it jumped nearly 5% — while the Nasdaq leapt 2.07% and the S&P 500 added 1.18%. With Brent crude steady near $72, the inflation worry that dogged June faded into the background.

AI DevelopmentModels & Compute

The open-weight race widens as Microsoft ships a model family and GLM-5.2 undercuts the frontier

Microsoft AI unveiled a family of seven new "MAI" models spanning image, voice, transcription, coding and reasoning — its clearest move yet to build a full in-house stack. In parallel, Zhipu's GLM-5.2 (744B-parameter MoE, MIT license, 1M-token context) reportedly beat GPT-5.5 on SWE-bench Pro at roughly one-seventh the output-token cost, sharpening the pressure on closed-model pricing.

▸ The fuller picture

The month's signal is competition on cost and openness rather than raw capability alone. Microsoft's seven-model MAI launch — described internally as a "hill-climbing machine" — pushes the company toward owning the model layer end to end instead of relying solely on partners, a notable strategic tell from one of the largest buyers and resellers of frontier compute.

The bigger price story is GLM-5.2. A capable mixture-of-experts model under a permissive MIT license, with a usable million-token context and benchmark results matching or beating a leading closed model at a fraction of the per-token cost, is exactly the kind of release that compresses margins across the industry. Moonshot's Kimi K2.7-Code, which cuts reasoning-token usage ~30%, points the same way: efficiency is becoming the battleground.

Hanging over all of it is a sharper geopolitical edge. The period also saw the first instance of a deployed commercial model pulled by state order under export-control rules — a reminder that as open weights proliferate, governments are increasingly treating frontier AI as strategic infrastructure to be gated, not just a product to be shipped.

New BreakthroughPhysics

Physicists coax ultracold atoms into a brand-new state of matter: the 'fractional Fermi sea'

Researchers report driving ultracold atoms into a previously unseen quantum phase they call a "fractional Fermi sea," in which the particles organise themselves in unexpected ways that go beyond the textbook picture. It hints at new rules for how matter can behave at the limits of low temperature and high density.

▸ The fuller picture

A "Fermi sea" is the standard description of how fermions — the particles that make up ordinary matter — fill up available energy states at near-absolute-zero temperatures. Finding a fractional version means the atoms settle into a collective arrangement that the conventional model doesn't predict, effectively a new phase of matter coaxed out of an ultracold atomic gas pushed to its physical limits.

Ultracold-atom systems are prized precisely because they are clean, tunable stand-ins for the messy quantum behaviour inside real materials. A new organising principle observed here can illuminate phenomena — exotic superconductivity, strange metals — that are hard to probe directly in solids, and the discoveries often feed forward into quantum-technology design.

It is early, fundamental science rather than anything with a near-term application, but it is the kind of result that expands the catalogue of what matter can do. June was a strong month for this flavour of physics, alongside advances in artificial photosynthesis and CO₂-to-methanol catalysis.

Something InterestingArchaeology

Scientists read 4,000-year-old letters without ever opening their sealed clay envelopes

Using advanced imaging, researchers decoded sealed cuneiform letters from ancient Anatolia without cracking the clay envelopes that have protected them for four millennia — reading private Bronze Age correspondence that no one was ever meant to see, all without destroying it.

▸ The fuller picture

In the ancient Near East, important clay tablets were sometimes sealed inside a second clay "envelope" — a tamper-evident wrapper that, once dried, could only be read by smashing it open. That left archaeologists with a brutal trade-off: destroy the artefact to read the message, or preserve it and never know what it said. Non-invasive scanning dissolves that dilemma, letting the text be reconstructed digitally while the object stays intact.

If you want a second curiosity for the commute: astronomers this month reported two of the lightest worlds ever found — a pair of Jupiter-sized planets so wispy they are less dense than candy floss, holding almost nothing inside. Between sealed letters and near-weightless giants, June was a good month for things that sound impossible but check out.
